Protect yourself from Phishing.

Some Phishing attempts will be more disguised than others with some being highly obvious that they’re not going to be doing what they say they are.

Here we see an example below, in which they are claiming that users can claim a “$50 Amazon Reward”, a lot of things are showing red flags about this – here are some of the main examples that I can pick out from this image:

  • E-Mail link (All e-mails from Amazon end in @Amazon.com).
  • No verification
  • Not regionally correct
  • ‘AmaZ0n’

Clicked a Phishing link? Be sure to do the following:

  • Disconnect the system from the Network.
  • Disable any wireless connectivity, including any systems such as Bluetooth (Should this be installed on your system).
  • Backup any files that are deemed as being important, if they aren't already. Preferrably this should be done through an offline medium, such as a USB Stick, or external hard drive.
  • Scan system for Malware, leave the system to take as long as it needs. This may vary dependent on the performance of the machine.
  • If you have another device that isn't contaminated with Malware, change passwords to your accounts from here. Consider using two-factor authentication if you haven't already (For more information on Passwords, click here).
  • Alert any contacts on your e-mail address about this. The Malware could have sent e-mails to others that are on your contacts list the same e-mail in an attempt to get more information.

If you haven't sent any information be sure to report it to ActionFraud. If you have sent any information be sure to report it as a Crime as soon as possible.

Remember

Understanding how these attacks are composed allow for you to gain a better insight as to how you can protect yourself, and others from these types of attacks.

Sophistication in stealing.

As of Q3 of 2018, almost 50% of phishing websites are using HTTPS encryption and are targeting around 286 brand names in their attacks.
